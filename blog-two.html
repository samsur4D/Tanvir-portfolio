Abstract

Ceramic tiles are widely utilized in construction for flooring and wall coverings due to their exceptional durability, aesthetic appeal, and versatility. However, the traditional production of these goods relies on limited resources, leading to environmental concerns and sustainability issues. As an abundant agricultural waste, coconut coir and husk can be an alternative raw material for tile production, as its ash contains valuable constituents such as SiO2, Al2O3, and CaO, among others, making it suitable for potentially reducing the industry's dependence on traditional resources such as silica and feldspar. This research investigates the possibility of utilizing coconut coir ash (0%, 5%, 15%, and 20%), a byproduct of the coconut industry, as a renewable and environmentally friendly resource for producing ceramic tiles. The water absorption rate was greatly reduced, especially for the 15% coconut coir ash batch. Also, a reduction in porosity was noticed within the increasing percentage of the ash in the composition. On the contrary, firing shrinkage showed a mixed result, and loss due to ignition increased proportionally with the increasing rate of the ash content. Regarding the physical characteristics, impact strength, compressive strength, tensile strength, and modulus of rupture increased with the rising CCA content, with the best results observed in the 15% batch.
